Visual sources

Maps

地図
kartat

Nine maps spanning the Edo period through the early twentieth century, tracing the geographic and administrative context of the Kawamura and Uratani families across Nara Prefecture and the Yamato basin. These images form the documentary foundation for reconstructing Hayashikōji-chō's urban geography and the hamlet of Ōkubochō in the landscape of the old Yamato heartland.

Note: These are high-resolution archival scans. For best viewing, open at full zoom or download to view at native resolution.

Edo and early Meiji context

Yamato meguri: Nara & Yoshino pilgrimage guide
1849
A pilgrim's guide to the sacred sites of the Yamato basin, centered on Nara and Mount Yoshino. This map shows the landscape as the Kawamura and Uratani families would have known it — the mountains, temples, and routes that structured rural life in the generation before modern administrative reorganization. Mount Unebi, the sacred hill at whose foot the Uratani compound sat, appears in the mountainous terrain to the southeast of Nara city.
Yamato no Kuni: Regional map with Nara detail
Meiji 12 (1879)
A detailed regional map of Yamato province in the early Meiji era, by the cartographer Tsutsui Shojiro. This shows the administrative divisions and settlements of the Kawamura and Uratani family territories — the old Takaichi-gun where the Uratani lived, and the urban center of Nara where the Kawamura were established. The map captures the landscape just as Keinosuke was entering adulthood.

The Meiji era: urban growth and administrative reorganization

Nara City guide: Sakata edition
Meiji 23 (1890)
A merchant's guide to Nara city in the early Meiji era, showing commercial establishments, streets, and neighborhoods. This is the Nara where Keinosuke grew up and where his father Jirōzō's generation maintained the family seat at Hayashikōji-chō. The map's level of street-by-street detail shows the urban fabric that has since been entirely rebuilt.
Nara City guide: Tsutsui edition
Meiji 29 (1896)
Another detailed Nara city guide, published six years later by Tsutsui Umekichi. The rapid development and urban change visible between the 1890 and 1896 editions reflects the modernization overtaking old merchant districts. This map is roughly contemporary with Keinosuke's coming of age.
Nara Prefecture administrative map
Meiji 35 (1902)
An official administrative map of Nara Prefecture showing the reorganized county (gun) and village (son) divisions following the turn of the century. By this date, Keinosuke was a young man, the Uratani compound had been absorbed into newly-constituted administrative units, and the old Takaichi-gun structure was giving way to modern local government. This map shows the legal geography within which the koseki were filed.
Nara Prefecture general map
Meiji 44 (1911)
A prefecture-wide map from 1911, showing the full extent of Nara and the outlying hamlets. At this date Keinosuke was about thirty, married to Toshie, and living in Osaka; his younger brother Kazuhide had not yet been born. The map shows the administrative grid within which his military conscription and deployment would be recorded.
Nara City map
Meiji 44 (1911)
A detailed map of Nara city proper in 1911. At this resolution and scale, the street layout and block structure that formed the backdrop to Hayashikōji-chō are visible — the old neighborhoods that Teruhiro would remember from childhood visits, the geography that shaped the family seat for generations. This map is contemporary with the period just before Keinosuke's war service.

The research focus: Hayashikōji-chō in detail

Nara City: tourist and commerce guide
Taisho 4 (1915)
This is the star of the archive's research collection. A beautifully hand-colored tourist and commerce guide to Nara from 1915 — right at the heart of the Meiji era when Keinosuke and Toshie were establishing their postwar life in Osaka. The map shows Hayashikōji-chō as a distinct neighborhood block with remarkable clarity: the three temples (妙善寺, 霊厳寺, 円証寺) appear as labeled landmarks within the block itself; nearby Isagawa-jinja is marked; the street layout and neighboring establishments are legible. This single map does more to anchor the family's history than any koseki entry can: it shows not just where the family lived, but what that place looked like, what its neighbors were, what its religious geography meant. The map is contemporary with the full flowering of the Meiji merchant city, before urban renewal of the postwar decades erased the neighborhoods entirely.

Historical context: the Edo baseline

Nara during the Edo period
Edo jidai (pre-1868)
A map of Nara as it existed during the Edo period, before the Meiji administrative reforms and urban modernization. This shows the settlement patterns, temple geography, and landscape of the world into which Jirōzō and Yoshi's children were born — the geographies that would persist, transformed, into the twentieth century. The continuity and discontinuity between this old Nara and the modern city visible in the 1915 map mark the historical span across which the family's story unfolds.
