Nara temple & urban geography

奈良寺院・地誌資料
tutkimusmuistiinpanot — archival reconstruction of Hayashikōji and the Kawamura family context

These materials form the core documentary basis for reconstructing the historical religious and urban environment surrounding the Kawamura family at Hayashikōji, in central Nara. The line of inquiry has shifted from surname-based genealogy toward the wider question of how the neighbourhood itself was organised — its temples, its blocks, its cemeteries — and how a family of the Kawamura's standing would have been embedded in that fabric.

The maternal side · Ōkubochō 大久保の寺社

The same household-to-temple precision that governs the Nara side resolves cleanly for the Uratani at Ōkubochō in Kashihara. The village danka temple is 国源寺 Kokugenji, a Jōdo-shū (Pure Land) foundation that is the surviving successor to the ancient Ōkubo-dera — a Hakuhō-period temple named in the Nihon Shoki under the year 686 — standing on the same ground. A few steps away is the village shrine, 大久保神社 Ōkubo-jinja, the hamlet's chinju. Given an extant Uratani nameplate at the ancestral address (modern 31-12 Ōkubochō), the family were almost certainly danka of Kokugenji and ujiko of the shrine for generations — which also settles, for the maternal line, that the affiliation was Pure Land and not Zen.

To obtain Magoichi's koseki, held by the Kashihara City office, would extend the Uratani line one generation further and should record Sumie's marriage — the notation naming which Kawamura household she married into, and so the identity of the "ojisan." Kokugenji's 過去帳, if it survives and is consultable, would carry posthumous names and dates for the earlier generations.

The Finnish side フィンランド側

Two threads run on the Hänninen side. The first is the dating of the Ålander → Jokimaa name change — a translation-fennicization rather than a phonetic one, almost certainly made during the great wave of Finnish surname changes in the decades around independence in 1917. Pinning the year, through parish records or the National Archives, would fix when — and in what nationalist climate — the family chose to be read as Finnish rather than Swedish-Finnish.

The second is the tradition of descent from Paavo Ruotsalainen (1777–1852). The claim deserves care: Paavo was a Savo peasant and the herännäisyys heartland was Savo and Ostrobothnia, yet the family attaches the claim to the Swedish-coastal Jokimaa/Ålander line — a mismatch that points more naturally to religious affiliation (the family as herännäiset, Awakening adherents) later hardened, over generations, into a claim of blood. The most direct next step is not an archive at all: it is to ask Armi directly what she was told, and by whom.
