The Finnish line

Hänninen

ハンニネン家
äidin suomalainen suku

The Hänninen were a large rural Finnish family — nine siblings born between the wars and into the immediate postwar years. The line carries within it, on the maternal side, a translated Swedish surname and a thread of family tradition reaching back to the lay preacher who founded one of the great currents of Finnish Lutheran piety.

Väinö Alfred Ilmari Hänninen grandfather

Maternal grandfather · head of the household into which Teruhiro married

In the Japanese marriage certificate his name was transliterated as ヴァイノ・アルフレッド・イルマリ・ハンニネン — three given names being the ordinary Finnish convention, though typically only the first, Väinö, would have been used in daily life. The name itself is a Finnish name from the Kalevala tradition (Väinämöinen, the old singer-shaman), made widely popular as a baptismal name during the Finnish national-romantic period at the turn of the twentieth century.

With his wife Toini he raised nine children in the Finnish countryside; the second-youngest of these would, in 1974, marry into the Kawamura line.

Toini Hänninen, née Jokimaa grandmother

Maternal grandmother · bearer of the Jokimaa name

Wife of Väinö, mother of the nine Hänninen siblings. Her maiden name Jokimaa — literally "river-land" — is the carrier of the family's most interesting genealogical thread. It is not the original surname of her line but a deliberate fennicization, a translation-of-style rather than of sound, made (as was common in early twentieth-century Finland) to replace an older Swedish surname: Ålander.

The change from Ålander to Jokimaa is not a phonetic adaptation — the two names sound nothing alike — and so it points back to a deliberate act of identity: a family choosing, at a particular moment in the rise of Finnish national consciousness, to be read as Finnish rather than as Swedish-Finnish. The original Ålander would have signalled descent from, or connection to, the Åland archipelago or its Swedish-speaking populations more broadly.

The Jokimaa name 川の地

The fennicization of Swedish surnames is one of the small visible legacies of the great Finnish national-romantic movement of the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. After centuries under the Swedish crown and then under Russian rule, the Finnish language and Finnish-language identity were elevated, in the decades surrounding independence in 1917, into a deliberate program of cultural assertion. Tens of thousands of families changed their names from Swedish to Finnish — sometimes by direct translation (e.g. Lindberg → Lehtimäki), sometimes by a freer transposition into a similar register of meaning, sometimes by abandoning the Swedish name altogether.

Ålander → Jokimaa belongs to the freer kind of transposition. The original name connected the family to Åland or to coastal Swedish-Finnish settlements; the chosen Finnish name evokes a parcel of land along a river, an inland landscape. The change records, in a single small act, the broader story of a Swedish-Finnish family making itself, on paper, Finnish.

Paavo Ruotsalainen 家伝

Family tradition on the Jokimaa side holds that the line reaches back to Paavo Ruotsalainen (1777–1852), the lay preacher and peasant mystic who became the central figure of herännäisyys — "the Awakening" — one of the four great pietist revival movements of Finnish Lutheranism, and the one most marked by a tradition of plain speech, inward struggle, and laypeople taking spiritual leadership from clergy.

Ruotsalainen was born in Savo, in eastern Finland, the son of a poor farmer; he was largely self-taught, but became known across the country as a spiritual counsellor whose preaching drew crowds from the surrounding parishes. His movement persisted long after his death, and remains an identifiable current within the Finnish Lutheran church today — recognizable by its plain dress, its hymn-singing tradition, and the annual Herättäjäjuhlat summer gatherings.

Whether the family connection is direct (a documented line of descent) or indirect (descent from a near relative or member of his early circle) has not yet been established. The tradition is worth recording as tradition; whether it will be possible to confirm it through Finnish parish records is among the open threads of this archive.

On the religious context The herännäisyys movement coexists with three other major Finnish pietist streams: the rukoilevaisuus ("Prayingites") of the southwest; the evankelisuus (Evangelicals) more aligned with the official church; and the Laestadians of the north. To be of heränneitten background is to belong to a particular history of Finnish religious culture, more inward and more lay-driven than the establishment Lutheran piety of the cities.

The nine siblings

Väinö and Toini raised nine children. One, Jorma, died in infancy and is remembered in the family roster but not the family life. The others — eight in all, born across roughly two decades from the 1930s into the 1950s — formed a large and characteristically Finnish rural sibling group, the kind that once made a small farm viable and a winter evening loud.

The names of those still living are kept off these pages out of respect for their privacy. Of the deceased, only Jorma is mentioned by name here, since he never had a chance to live a life of his own to safeguard.

Among the names in the roster appears the older Nordic spelling Alise rather than the more modern Alice — a small linguistic trace, consistent with the family's Swedish-side heritage and the conservatism of rural naming.

The joining

In January 1974, in Finland, the eighth-born of the Hänninen children — Armi Annikki — married Kawamura Teruhiro, who had arrived in Europe a few years earlier after his work at Expo '70 in Osaka. The marriage was registered first in Finland and then, through the Japanese embassy, in the koseki at Osaka; their first child was born in April of the same year. Teruhiro, though, kept his own name rather than taking his wife's; the Kawamura line did not end at this joining but emigrated through it, the name passing to his children in their new country.

The name Hänninen is, like all surnames, a partial story of where a family has been — the Jokimaa marriage carrying Ålander's older trace, the Hänninen patrilineal name carrying its own, the Kawamura family's name in turn carried out of Japan into another language by Teruhiro. Three layers of inheritance, written one over the other.
