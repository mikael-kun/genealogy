The documents 戸籍

The primary sources for the Japanese side of this archive are koseki — family registers. The koseki is a household-based civil registration document, in continuous use since 1872, that records every birth, marriage, divorce, adoption, change of domicile, and death within a household. Before the 1948 reform of the family law, a single register could span three or four generations of an extended household; after the reform, registers were rewritten in a simpler form covering only a nuclear family.

The pre-reform register was called the 改製原戸籍 (kaisei genkoseki) — literally "the original register before revision". It is these older documents, written in vertical brushwork with the kanji numerals 壹貳參拾 and stamped with the small red 印 of the receiving official, that hold the deepest record. The koseki used as sources here are reproductions obtained from the Nishinomiya City Hall in May 2026.

The Finnish side rests, for the moment, more on oral tradition than on documents. Finland keeps its own parish-based and civil-registry records — the Suomen Sukututkimusseura (Finnish Genealogical Society) holds digitized parish registers for most of the country reaching back to the eighteenth century — and these will be the next sources to consult. The names, surname history, and Ruotsalainen tradition recorded on the Hänninen page are family knowledge as transmitted; the documentary work remains to be done.

What the koseki records Names, family relationships, exact dates of birth and death, addresses of household seat, marriage and divorce events, and the chain of transfers between municipalities. By Japanese law these registers must be retained by the holding city for 150 years (extended from 80 in 2010), so older closed registers can usually still be retrieved by direct descendants on application.
What the koseki does not record Occupation, religion, military service, photographs, character, voice, the smell of a house. For these one has to rely on what the living remember.

On reading the brushwork

The handwritten pre-war registers are not always easy to parse. A few conventions worth noting:

Numbers appear in the old "anti-fraud" form: 壹 for one, 貳 for two, 參 for three, 拾 for ten. So 大正拾参年拾壹月貳拾五日 reads as Taishō 13, eleventh month, twenty-fifth day — 25 November 1924.

Women's given names are commonly written in katakana rather than kanji, reflecting a generation in which formal female education was limited and in which a woman might primarily write her own name in kana. The convention shifted by the postwar generation, when girls' names came routinely to be registered in kanji.

Given-name readings are frequently nanori — special personal-name readings unrelated to the dictionary reading of the kanji. 信一 is read here as Nobukazu, not Shin'ichi. 憲英 is Kazuhide, not Ken'ei. These cannot be deduced from the characters alone; they must come from the family.

The red circular stamps along the margins are the 印 (in) of the municipal officials who received, forwarded, and registered each event. They are the bureaucratic equivalent of a fingerprint on a clay tablet — proof that a particular person, in a particular office, on a particular day, set the matter down as official.

On family memory

Where a fact rests only on family recollection rather than on a document, the archive notes as much. The principal oral source is 河村照博 Teruhiro, who supplied the readings of names, the identity of Sumie as keeper of the Ōkubo house, the structure of the doubled Kawamura-Uratani marriage, the existence and the Sakai location of the family kaku-uchi shop, the single conversation in which Keinosuke admitted to having been in Manchukuo, and the irreplaceable detail of how the houses at Hayashikōji-chō smelled in summer.

The compilation of these pages is itself a kind of memorial work, made possible by the convergence of documents that survived seventy years of upheaval, an old man's reluctant disclosure twenty years ago, and a city-hall counter clerk in Nishinomiya willing to hand over a stack of reproductions in May 2026.

Open threads

Several leads remain to be followed:

On the Japanese side

The koseki of Kawamura Nobukazu, which would name Jirōzō's own parents and reach one generation further into the nineteenth century — and crucially would name all of his brothers, identifying which one married Sumie — is held by the Nara City Office. Obtaining it is among the highest-leverage next steps.

The koseki of Uratani Magoichi, similarly, is held by the Kashihara City Office (which absorbed the old Unebi-chō in 1956). It should name his parents and may clarify the Uratani descent.

The military service record (兵籍簿) of Keinosuke, if it survives, would be held by Nara Prefecture, since his permanent domicile at the end of the war in August 1945 was Hayashikōji-chō. The record would name his regiment, his rank, his deployment to Manchukuo, and the circumstances of his return. Whether the family will wish to read it is a separate question.

A household register for the Kawamura uncle who married Sumie may also be obtainable from the Sakai City Office, since the family's kaku-uchi shop was located there and a separate koseki would likely have been established for his branch.

A separate and increasingly substantial line of inquiry has opened around the temple geography of Hayashikōji itself: the Meiji-era 寺院明細帳 records at least three temple establishments within the block (妙善寺, 霊厳寺, 円証寺), and the Kawamura family of No. 7 would almost certainly have been a 檀家 (parish household) of one of them. If the temple can be identified, its mortuary register (過去帳) would extend the family record several generations further than the koseki alone.

On the Finnish side

The Paavo Ruotsalainen connection on the Jokimaa side is among the most evocative open threads, and one of the harder to confirm. The first step would be a direct conversation with the oldest surviving Hänninen siblings, who may remember which branch of the family the tradition attaches to. From there, Finnish parish records — held by the Finnish National Archives and the National Library's digital archives — can in principle be searched back to the early nineteenth century.

The original Ålander form of the Jokimaa surname also bears tracing. Swedish-Finnish surnames of this kind often have parish-archive trails reaching the eighteenth century or earlier.
