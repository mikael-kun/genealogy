The paternal line

Kawamura

河村家
isän puoli

The Kawamura family seat stood at No. 7 Hayashikōji-chō, in the old center of Nara. The family produced at least six sons in the late Taishō years; the eldest kept the house, and the others made their own way. Two of those younger brothers married into the same Uratani household one generation apart, binding the two families into a doubled tie.

河村 治郎造Kawamura Jirōzō

Great-grandfather · father of at least six sons

Husband of Yoshi. Nothing more is presently known. The earliest register in family hands records Jirōzō only as the father of his children; his own koseki, which would name his parents in turn, remains to be obtained from the Nara City office.

河村 よしKawamura Yoshi

Great-grandmother · mother of at least six sons

Wife of Jirōzō. Her given name is recorded in hiragana — a common convention for women of her generation, when daughters were often registered in kana rather than kanji. To borrow Teruhiro's framing: women in those years were not always expected to write their own names in the harder script.

河村 信一Kawamura Nobukazu

Great-uncle · eldest son · "Nobu-san"

The eldest of Jirōzō and Yoshi's sons, and therefore the heir to the family headship at Hayashikōji-chō. Keinosuke transferred out of Nobukazu's koseki on marriage, which is the standard pattern: a non-inheriting son leaves the elder brother's register to establish his own. Within the family Nobukazu was known by the affectionate Nobu-san — a reading not always obvious from the kanji of his given name.

河村 ◯◯a Kawamura brother, name not yet established

Great-uncle · one of the middle sons · husband of Uratani Sumie

One of Jirōzō and Yoshi's sons — not Nobukazu, and not Keinosuke — married Sumie, the elder Uratani daughter, in an arranged match sometime before the war. His name is not yet established; the family knows of him only as ojisan (uncle), without specifying which of the middle brothers he was.

It was his marriage that first brought the Kawamura and the Uratani together as kin, several years before Keinosuke and Toshie's own union. He and Sumie ran the family 角打ち kaku-uchi shop in Sakai — see below — and their household became one of the social centers of the extended family in those years.

To do Identifying this uncle by name is among the more pressing open questions. The Nara koseki of Nobukazu, when obtained, should list all of his brothers and thus narrow the field. The Sakai City office may also have a household register for him, since the kaku-uchi was located there.

河村 敬之介Kawamura Keinosuke

Grandfather · sixth son · 20 December 1921 — 11 August 2004

Born in Nara at the close of 1921, Keinosuke was the sixth son in his family and so was never going to inherit the house at Hayashikōji-chō. As a young man he was conscripted into the Imperial Japanese Army and sent to Manchukuo, the Japanese puppet state in northeastern China, where he served until the war's end.

He spoke of the war reluctantly, and at length only once, to a grandson who was asking about military service of a more modern sort. What he said was little: that he had been in Manchukuo; that he had already known the woman he would marry before he shipped out. That she was the younger sister of his elder brother's wife — that the families were already kin — accounts for how that earlier acquaintance came about. When the grandson mentioned having served, by chance, in a military-police unit during his own Finnish conscription, Keinosuke recoiled with visible disgust — a small but telling reaction from a man who had seen what the wartime 憲兵 kenpeitai meant up close, and had not been one of them.

He must have returned from the continent by the spring of 1947 at the latest, since his eldest son was born in February of 1948. Many of his contemporaries from the Kwantung Army did not come home at all, or returned only after years of Siberian internment. His own route home is not recorded.

He married Toshie on 10 February 1948 — the same day his first son was born, in the Uratani family compound where Toshie had returned to give birth. The family lived for many years in the Imazato district of Osaka. Keinosuke died in Bangkok in the summer of 2004, four weeks short of the first anniversary of Toshie's death in Osaka the year before.

On the silence Reticence about the war was nearly universal in Keinosuke's generation, and especially common among Manchukuo and Siberian returnees, for whom the entire experience carried both personal trauma and a complicated postwar political weight. That he spoke at all, even briefly, was a kind of gift.

河村 照博Kawamura Teruhiroliving

Father · eldest son · born 10 February 1948

Born on 10 February 1948 in the Uratani family compound at Ōkubo — the same day his parents legalized their marriage. He spent his early years in the Imazato district of Osaka, escaping when he could to the old Uratani house in Ōkubo, where his aunt Sumie still lived, and to the kaku-uchi shop in Sakai, where the two strands of the extended family met daily over the counter and where he trained judo with his double cousins.

He emigrated to Finland in the early 1970s, married there in January 1974, and in time took Finnish citizenship and his wife's surname. His own register at 林小路町七番地 was struck and a new family register was compiled in Osaka — at the address that had become the family's mainstay in Shin-Imazato — before the move abroad became permanent.

Most of what is known in this archive about the generation above — the smell of the old Hayashikōji-chō houses, Sumie's role as keeper of the Uratani home, the doubled marriage that bound the two families together, the small details of how Nobukazu was actually called — has come from his memory.

河村 憲英Kawamura Kazuhide

Uncle · second son · born 27 December 1951 - ◯◯.◯◯.2005

Younger brother of Teruhiro by nearly four years, born at the family home in Shin-Imazato, Osaka. He remained in Japan and became, in the way that younger siblings often do when the elder one has gone abroad, the family's anchor in Osaka — registering both his parents' deaths a year apart in 2003 and 2004.

The Sakai shop 堺の角打ち

Among the meeting places of the extended family in the postwar decades was a 角打ち kaku-uchi in the city of Sakai, south of Osaka — a small standing-counter sake-and-beer shop run by the Kawamura uncle who had married Sumie. The institution itself is a particularly Kansai one: kaku-uchi grew out of the practice of liquor wholesalers letting customers drink at the corner of the shop, glass in hand, no chair offered, no kitchen necessarily attached. The name itself means simply "drinking at the corner."

The shop served as a daily node where the Kawamura and Uratani kin lines, already entwined by two marriages, also crossed economically — uncles, aunts, and cousins working the counter, making deliveries, and stopping in. Teruhiro retains memories of being taken on deliveries by an older cousin from this side of the family. The shop is no longer there, but it gave the families a shared rhythm of work and visitation that the koseki cannot reach.

The two unions
The Kawamura–Uratani alliance was made twice. First, in an arranged marriage between Sumie (eldest Uratani daughter) and one of the middle Kawamura brothers, before the war. Second, when Keinosuke (sixth Kawamura son) married Toshie (Sumie's younger sister) in February 1948. The children of the second marriage and the children of the first were therefore double first cousins — related on both the paternal and maternal lines independently — and grew up unusually close.
