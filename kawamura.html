The paternal line

Kawamura

河村家
isän puoli

The Kawamura family seat stood at No. 7 Hayashikōji-chō, in the old center of Nara, a short walk east of what is today the Japan Railways station. The family produced at least six sons in the late Taishō years; this page records the two whose lives intersect with the rest of this archive.

河村 治郎造Kawamura Jirōzō

Great-grandfather · father of Keinosuke

Husband of Yoshi, father of at least six sons. Nothing more is presently known. The earliest register in family hands records Jirōzō only as the father of his children; his own koseki, which would name his parents in turn, remains to be obtained from the Nara City office.

河村 よしKawamura Yoshi

Great-grandmother · mother of Keinosuke

Wife of Jirōzō. Her given name is recorded in hiragana — a common convention for women of her generation, when daughters were often registered in kana rather than kanji. To borrow Teruhiro's framing: women in those years were not always expected to write their own names in the harder script.

河村 信一Kawamura Nobukazu

Great-uncle · eldest son · "Nobu-san"

The eldest of Jirōzō and Yoshi's sons, and therefore the heir to the family headship at Hayashikōji-chō. Keinosuke transferred out of Nobukazu's koseki on marriage, which is the standard pattern: a non-inheriting son leaves the elder brother's register to establish his own. Within the family Nobukazu was known by the affectionate Nobu-san — a reading not always obvious from the kanji of his given name.

河村 敬之介Kawamura Keinosuke

Grandfather · sixth son · 20 December 1921 — 11 August 2004

Born in Nara at the close of 1921, Keinosuke was the sixth son in his family and so was never going to inherit the house at Hayashikōji-chō. As a young man he was conscripted into the Imperial Japanese Army and sent to Manchukuo, the Japanese puppet state in northeastern China, where he served until the war's end.

He spoke of the war reluctantly, and at length only once, to a grandson who was asking about military service of a more modern sort. What he said was little: that he had been in Manchukuo; that he had already known the woman he would marry before he shipped out. When the grandson mentioned having served, by chance, in a military-police unit during his own Finnish conscription, Keinosuke recoiled with visible disgust — a small but telling reaction from a man who had seen what the wartime 憲兵 kenpeitai meant up close, and had not been one of them.

He must have returned from the continent by the spring of 1947 at the latest, since his eldest son was born in February of 1948. Many of his contemporaries from the Kwantung Army did not come home at all, or returned only after years of Siberian internment. His own route home is not recorded.

He married Toshie on 10 February 1948 — the same day his first son was born, in the Uratani family compound where Toshie had returned to give birth. The family lived for many years in the Imazato district of Osaka. Keinosuke died in Bangkok in the summer of 2004, four weeks short of the first anniversary of Toshie's death in Osaka the year before.

On the silence Reticence about the war was nearly universal in Keinosuke's generation, and especially common among Manchukuo and Siberian returnees, for whom the entire experience carried both personal trauma and a complicated postwar political weight. That he spoke at all, even briefly, was a kind of gift.

河村 照博Kawamura Teruhiroliving

Father · eldest son · born 10 February 1948

Born on 10 February 1948 in the Uratani family compound at Ōkubo — the same day his parents legalized their marriage. He emigrated to Finland in the early 1970s, married there in 1974, and in due course took Finnish citizenship and his wife's surname. His own register at 林小路町七番地 was struck and a new family register was compiled in Osaka, where the new household was on paper based, before the move abroad became permanent.

Most of what is known in this archive about the generation above — the smell of the old Hayashikōji-chō houses, Sumie's role as keeper of the Uratani home, the small details about how Nobukazu was actually called — has come from his memory.

河村 憲英Kawamura Kazuhideliving

Uncle · second son · born 27 December 1951

Younger brother of Teruhiro by nearly four years, born at the family home in Shin-Imazato, Osaka. He remained in Japan and became, in the way that younger siblings often do when the elder one has gone abroad, the family's anchor in Osaka — registering both his parents' deaths a year apart in 2003 and 2004.
