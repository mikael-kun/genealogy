The paternal line

Kawamura

河村家
isän puoli

The Kawamura family seat stood at No. 7 Hayashikōji-chō, in the old center of Nara. The family produced at least six sons in the late Taishō years; the eldest kept the house, and the others made their own way. Two of those younger brothers married into the same Uratani household, binding the two families into a doubled tie.

河村 治郎造Kawamura Jirōzō

Great-grandfather · father of at least six sons

Husband of Yoshi. Nothing more is presently known. The earliest register in family hands records Jirōzō only as the father of his children; his own koseki, which would name his parents in turn, remains to be obtained from the Nara City office.

河村 よしKawamura Yoshi

Great-grandmother · mother of at least six sons

Wife of Jirōzō. Her given name is recorded in hiragana — a common convention for women of her generation, when daughters were often registered in kana rather than kanji.

河村 信一Kawamura Nobu ◯

Great-uncle · eldest son · "Nobu-san"

The eldest of Jirōzō and Yoshi's sons, and therefore the heir to the family headship at Hayashikōji-chō. Nobu-san became the head of the family after Jirōzō passed away. Keinosuke transferred out of Nobu◯'s koseki on marriage; a non-inheriting son leaves the family register to establish his own. Within the family Nobu◯ was known by the affectionate Nobu-san — a reading not always obvious from the kanji of his given name.

河村 ◯◯a Kawamura brother, name not yet established

Great-uncle · one of the middle sons · husband of Uratani Sumie (?)

One of Jirōzō and Yoshi's sons — not Nobu-san, and not Keinosuke — married Sumie(?), the elder Uratani daughter, in an arranged match sometime before the war. His name is not yet established; the family knows of him only as ojisan (uncle), without specifying which of the middle brothers he was.

It was his marriage that first brought the Kawamura and the Uratani together as kin, before Keinosuke and Toshie's own union. He and Sumie(?) ran the family 角打ち kaku-uchi shop in Sakai — see below — and their household became one of the social centers of the extended family in those years.

To do Identifying this uncle by name is among the more pressing open questions. The Nara koseki of 河村 信一, when obtained, should list all of his brothers and thus narrow the field. The Sakai City office may also have a household register for him, since the kaku-uchi was located there.

河村 敬之介Kawamura Keinosuke

Grandfather · sixth son · 20 December 1921 — 11 August 2004

Born in Nara at the close of 1921, Keinosuke was the sixth son in his family and so was never going to inherit the house at Hayashikōji-chō. As a young man he was conscripted into the Imperial Japanese Army and sent to Manchukuo, the Japanese puppet state in northeastern China, where he served until the war's end.

He spoke of the war reluctantly, and at length only once, to a grandson who was talking about military service of a more modern sort. What he said was little: that he had been in Manchukuo. When the grandson mentioned having served, by chance, in a military-police unit during his own Finnish conscription, Keinosuke recoiled with visible disgust — a small but telling reaction from a man who had seen what the wartime 憲兵 kenpeitai meant up close, and had not been one of them.

He must have returned from the continent by the spring of 1947 at the latest, since his eldest son was born in February of 1948. Many of his contemporaries from the Manchukuo did not come home at all, or returned only after years of Siberian internment. His own route home is not recorded.

He married Toshie on 10 February 1948 — the same day his first son was born, in the Uratani family compound where Toshie had returned to give birth. The family lived for many years in the Imazato district of Osaka. In the years after the 1995 Hanshin earthquake, as housing costs across the region rose sharply, the rent at Imazato grew beyond what the couple could carry. Toshie asked whether they might join their elder son's family in Finland; in the end they did not, and gave up the Osaka home for Himeji instead. When Toshie died in the summer of 2003 in an Osaka hospital, of cancer Keinosuke left Japan for his younger son's household in Bangkok, and died there about a month after the first anniversary of Toshie's death, on 11 August 2004. The circumstances of that final year are set down below.

On the silence Reticence about the war was nearly universal in Keinosuke's generation, and especially common among Manchukuo and Siberian returnees, for whom the entire experience carried both personal trauma and a complicated postwar political weight. That he spoke at all, even briefly, was a kind of gift.

河村 照博Kawamura Teruhiroliving

Father · eldest son · born 10 February 1948

Born on 10 February 1948 in the Uratani family compound at Ōkubo — the same day his parents legalized their marriage. He spent his early years in the Imazato district of Osaka, escaping when he could to the old Uratani house in Ōkubo, where his aunt Sumie still lived, and to the kaku-uchi shop in Sakai, where the two strands of the extended family met daily over the counter, where he trained judo with his double cousins.

What he wanted was to paint. Toshie would not have it: she set him a choice between law and economics and a finished degree, and he met her halfway — taking a bachelor's in macroeconomics at Kansai University before turning back toward the thing he had meant to do all along. To fund the leaving, he worked at Expo '70, the Osaka world's fair, saving against a journey west.

He went overland. The route ran clear across the Soviet Union on the Trans-Siberian railway, and Finland was where the train let him out — the first Western country at the far end of the crossing. His true destination was Paris, and there he studied art; afterward came a year or so in London, keeping the household of a former British colonel near Hyde Park, before he turned back north. The Nara painter's son had reached the West by the long way round.

He married Armi Hänninen in Finland in January 1974, a few months before their first child was born that spring. Armi took his name, becoming Armi Kawamura. His register at 林小路町七番地 was struck and a new family register compiled in Osaka, at the Shin-Imazato address that had become the family's mainstay.

Their life would run between the two countries. In 1978, soon after the birth of their second daughter, Teruhiro and Armi set out for Japan with the children — and the getting there became a small family epic. They sailed by ferry from Helsinki to Lübeck, took the train south to Frankfurt, and waited there for a flight to Singapore that never came. Rebooked onto the next plane, they lost their onward connection to Osaka: they were still in the air when Kazuhide stood waiting at the Osaka airport for a family that would not arrive, and they spent an unplanned night in Singapore before the airline could put them on the next flight in.

They came to rest first for a few months in Shin-Imazato, with Keinosuke and Toshie, and then in a home of their own in the Kurakuen district of Nishinomiya, in the hills between Osaka and Kobe. It is there that the family's household register settled — which is why the koseki at the foundation of this archive were drawn, decades later, from the Nishinomiya City Hall. They stayed until 1984, when — the life of a Japanese salaryman having proved an ill fit — they turned back to Finland: first to Espoo, and from December 1986 to Simonkylä in Vantaa, where the family settled for good.

Most of what is known in this archive about the generation above — the smell of the old Hayashikōji-chō houses, Sumie's role as keeper of the Uratani home, the doubled marriage that bound the two families together, the small details of how Nobukazu was actually called — has come from his memory.

河村 憲英Kawamura Kazuhide

Uncle · second son · 27 December 1951 — 2005

Younger brother of Teruhiro by nearly four years, born at the family home in Shin-Imazato, Osaka. While his elder brother's life ran between Finland and Japan, Kazuhide stayed the family's anchor in Japan — at Minami-morimachi by 1998, later at Kema-chō — before moving to Bangkok with his second family. It was he who registered both his parents' deaths, a year apart, in 2003 and 2004; and it was to his Bangkok household that Keinosuke came after Toshie's death. His own death there, in 2005, closes the account told below.

The Sakai shop 堺の角打ち

Among the meeting places of the extended family in the postwar decades was a 角打ち kaku-uchi in the city of Sakai, south of Osaka — a small standing-counter sake-and-beer shop run by the Kawamura uncle who had married Sumie. The institution itself is a particularly Kansai one: kaku-uchi grew out of the practice of liquor wholesalers letting customers drink at the corner of the shop, glass in hand, no chair offered, no kitchen necessarily attached. The name itself means simply "drinking at the corner."

The shop served as a daily node where the Kawamura and Uratani kin lines, already entwined by two marriages, also crossed economically — uncles, aunts, and cousins working the counter, making deliveries, and stopping in. Teruhiro retains memories of being taken on deliveries by an older cousin from this side of the family. The shop is no longer there, but it gave the families a shared rhythm of work and visitation that the koseki cannot reach.

The two unions
The Kawamura–Uratani alliance was made twice. First, in an arranged marriage between Sumie (eldest Uratani daughter) and one of the middle Kawamura brothers, before the war. Second, when Keinosuke (sixth Kawamura son) married Toshie (Sumie's younger sister) in February 1948. The children of the second marriage and the children of the first were therefore double first cousins — related on both the paternal and maternal lines independently — and grew up unusually close.

The Imazato shoe shop 今里の靴屋

The Imazato home was a workplace as much as a household: Keinosuke and Toshie kept a shoemaker's shop there, in Shin-Imazato, and it is the shop more than the rooms that Teruhiro remembers — the leather shoes ranged along the walls, the smell and weight of the hides, the bench where the work was done. It was a craftsman's trade, of a different order from the standing-counter shop the Sakai branch ran, and it was the family's own.

The shop reached forward into the son. It was there that Teruhiro learned to sew, well enough that as a young man he made his own clothes — the same patience of the hands that would later want an easel. Seen from the shoe-bench, the painter he set out across Siberia to become is less a break from the family trade than its continuation by other means: leather, then cloth, then paint.

To do A shoemaker's shop is concrete enough to look for. Postwar Osaka commercial directories — 商工名鑑 — and the Ikuno-ku ward listings of the late 1950s and 1960s may carry a Kawamura entry at the Shin-Imazato address, which would be the first time this family surfaces in a business record rather than a koseki.

The last years バンコク

When Toshie died in the summer of 2003, Keinosuke did not stay on in Japan. His younger son, Kazuhide, had by then settled in Bangkok with his second wife and their young son, and it was into that household that Keinosuke moved for what proved to be the final year of his life. The Himeji property was sold, and its proceeds were given by Keinosuke to Kazuhide, to provide for his keeping; on the Thai side, three houses had been built for members of the family.

In August of 2004 Kazuhide telephoned his brother in Finland to say that their father had been found dead at the foot of a staircase. Teruhiro travelled to Bangkok. The funeral lasted three days, in the Thai manner, with substantial offerings made to the monks and to the temple. What remained of Keinosuke's estate passed to Kazuhide.

It did not take long, after the funeral, for the household to come apart. Kazuhide's wife left him, taking their son; neither has been heard from since. He found himself shut out of the house — and as a foreign national he could hold no property in Thailand in his own name, and so had no standing to ask the authorities for help. He went to stay with a friend. He did not come back from the year that had passed, and died in Bangkok in 2005.

When Teruhiro and his son went to confirm the body, what was left of Kazuhide came to three things: a car, a video camera, and his wallet.

On what remains unclear Much about this last year has never been settled, and the family does not pretend otherwise. The sequence of it — the inheritance drawn to one side, the household dissolving the moment the elder man was gone — has left questions that cannot now be answered, and a grief that does not resolve into any clean account. They are set down here as they are known, and left at that.
