The maternal line

Uratani

浦谷家
äidin puoli

The Uratani lived at house No. 32 in Ōaza Ōkubo, a hamlet within Unebi-chō in the old Takaichi-gun, deep in the Yamato basin at the foot of Mount Unebi — one of the three sacred hills of the Asuka heartland, where the imperial mausolea of the earliest emperors stand. The hamlet was absorbed into Kashihara City in 1956. The kanji of the family name, 浦谷, mean "the bay of the valley" — an inland family with a coastal name.

浦谷 孫一Uratani Magoichi

Maternal great-grandfather · deceased before February 1948

Father of Sumie and Toshie. By the time of Toshie's marriage in early 1948 his name in the koseki carries the prefix 亡 — "the late" — meaning he had already died, though the precise year is not yet established. His own koseki, which would name his parents and extend the line one generation further back, is held by the Kashihara City office and remains to be requested.

浦谷 ミノヱUratani Minoe

Maternal great-grandmother · mother of Sumie and Toshie

Widow of Magoichi by 1948. Her name, like her daughters', is recorded in katakana, ending in the older kana ヱ — the historic we syllable that has since fallen out of use but which corresponds in modern writing to the kanji 江 ("inlet"). The three women of the Uratani household — Minoe, Sumie, and Toshie — all share this -江 ending, a typical Meiji/Taishō naming convention for women in Kansai families.

浦谷 スミエUratani Sumie

Great-aunt · elder daughter · the first Kawamura–Uratani bridge

Toshie's elder sister, by family account not by very many years. Sumie's marriage to one of the middle Kawamura brothers — an arranged match — was the first of two Kawamura–Uratani unions, and it brought the two houses into kin relation before her younger sister would do the same. That earlier alliance is why Keinosuke and Toshie "already knew each other" before he was sent to Manchukuo; they had been brought together as in-laws long before they were married to each other.

The household she made with her Kawamura husband ran a 角打ち kaku-uchi shop in Sakai, south of Osaka — a small standing-counter sake-and-beer establishment that became, in the postwar decades, one of the gathering places of the extended family. Yet she also kept, or returned to keep, the old Uratani house at Ōkubo: it was to her there, in the countryside of Nara, that her young nephew Teruhiro would escape from the density of postwar Osaka.

The result of that doubled connection — Sumie + Kawamura brother, then Toshie + Keinosuke — was that the cousins of the next generation grew up as double first cousins, related on both the paternal and maternal sides independently. In practice this meant a closeness most cousins do not have. Teruhiro and his cousin Yasuhiro were inseparable as boys — they trained judo together — and in their teens it was the older Yoshiaki who let the two of them tag along on the shop's delivery runs in Sakai.

浦谷 トシヱUratani Toshie

Grandmother · second daughter · 25 November 1924 — 8 July 2003

Born in the family compound at Ōkubo in late November 1924, the second daughter of Magoichi and Minoe. Her elder sister Sumie married into the Kawamura house at Hayashikōji-chō meant that her own match with another Kawamura brother — Keinosuke — was the closing of an arrangement, not the opening of one. Whether she met him at family gatherings of the in-laws, or through a more formal pre-war omiai, the koseki does not say.

She and Keinosuke married on 10 February 1948. That same day, in the same hamlet — in the house next door at Ōkubo No. 31, in fact — she gave birth to their eldest son. The pattern is a classic 里帰り出産 (satogaeri shussan): an expectant mother returning to her own family's home to deliver. The simultaneous filing of the marriage and the imminent birth is unusual to modern eyes, but in the disrupted years immediately after the war it carried less weight than the simple fact of his having come back at all.

She lived another fifty-five years, dying in Osaka in July 2003. Keinosuke followed thirteen months later.

A doubled tie
The Kawamura–Uratani alliance was made twice. First, in an arranged marriage between Sumie (eldest Uratani daughter) and one of the middle Kawamura brothers, before the war. Second, when Keinosuke (sixth Kawamura son) married Toshie (Sumie's younger sister) in February 1948. The cousins of the next generation were therefore double first cousins — kin on both the paternal and maternal lines.
