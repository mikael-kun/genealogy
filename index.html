A family archive

Kawamura · Uratani · Hänninen

河村家 ・ 浦谷家 ・ ハンニネン家
kolmen suvun kirja

These pages gather what can be known of three families joined across two continents — the Kawamura of Nara, the Uratani of Ōkubo in the old Yamato basin, and the Hänninen of rural Finland — as recorded in surviving family registers and as remembered by those who knew them.

The archive begins with the generation born in the late Meiji and Taishō eras, when Japanese names for daughters were still commonly inscribed in katakana, and ends, for now, at the threshold of the present. The two Japanese families were already entwined before they were joined in the second of two marriages — and the line travelled west, in 1974, to a Finnish farming village whose own name carries an older, Swedish trace.

The primary documents are koseki — handwritten family registers, brush-drawn in vertical columns with red 印 seals along the margins. They record births, marriages, deaths, and movements between municipalities. What they do not record — affections, silences, the smell of a house in summer — has been supplied where possible by the memory of those still living.

A doubled tie

The arrangement at the centre of this tree is unusual and important. The Kawamura and the Uratani were joined by two marriages in two generations of the Kawamura brothers — meaning that the children of Keinosuke and Toshie were not just first cousins of the children of the Sumie marriage on both sides at once. They were double first cousins: kin through two independent lines of descent. In Yamato families of that era, such doubled alliances were not unheard of; they tied two houses together more firmly than any single marriage could.

The first marriage — an arranged one, between Sumie and a Kawamura brother whose identity remains to be confirmed — established the alliance. The second — between Keinosuke and Toshie — closed it in 1948. That Keinosuke and Toshie "already knew each other" before his deployment to Manchukuo, as he once admitted in passing, is no longer surprising; the families had been one for years.

The three families

The Kawamura were a Nara family whose seat sat at 林小路町七番地 — Hayashikōji-chō No. 7, in the old center of the city. Keinosuke was their sixth son, which is to say that the family land and headship were never going to be his; the eldest brother Nobukazu kept the house. Other brothers, including the one who married Sumie, set out to make their own way — in his case running a 角打ち kaku-uchi standing-counter shop in Sakai, south of Osaka.

The Uratani lived in 大字大久保 — Ōaza Ōkubo, a hamlet in Unebi-chō within Takaichi-gun, now absorbed into the city of Kashihara. Their compound stood at house number 32, with the next-door house at 31 holding family of the same kin group. It was at number 31 that the first child of the next generation was born.

The Hänninen were a large rural Finnish family — nine siblings born between the wars — whose name on the maternal side, Jokimaa, was itself a translation: a deliberate fennicization of an older Swedish surname. Through them runs a thread of family lore connecting back to the early-nineteenth-century lay preacher Paavo Ruotsalainen and the religious awakening he led.

The Japanese and Finnish families joined in January 1974, when Teruhiro — the firstborn of Keinosuke and Toshie's marriage and the first of his generation to leave Japan — married into the Hänninen line in Finland.
