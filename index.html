A family archive

Kawamura & Uratani

河村家 ・ 浦谷家
Kawamuran ja Uratanin suvut

These pages gather what can be known of two families from the old Yamato basin — the Kawamura of Nara and the Uratani of Ōkubo, near the foot of Mount Unebi — as recorded in the family registers held at the Nishinomiya City Hall and as remembered by those who knew them.

The archive begins with the generation born in the late Meiji and Taishō eras, when Japanese names for daughters were still commonly inscribed in katakana, and ends, for now, at the threshold of the present. It is offered in three languages because the family itself came to live in three: Japanese, English, and Finnish.

The primary documents are koseki — handwritten family registers, brush-drawn in vertical columns with red 印 seals along the margins. They record births, marriages, deaths, and movements between municipalities. What they do not record — affections, silences, the smell of a house in summer — has been supplied where possible by the memory of those still living.

The family 家系

Documented kin born before circa 1960. Living descendants of the next generation are omitted from this public archive.

The two houses

The Kawamura were a Nara family whose seat sat at 林小路町七番地 — Hayashikōji-chō No. 7, in the old center of the city. Keinosuke was their sixth son, which is to say that the family land and headship were never going to be his.

The Uratani lived in 大字大久保 — Ōaza Ōkubo, a hamlet in Unebi-chō within Takaichi-gun, now absorbed into the city of Kashihara. Their compound stood at house number 32, with the next-door house at 31 holding family of the same kin group. It was at number 31 that the first child of the next generation was born.

The two families joined in February 1948, three years after the war's end, by which time the bride and groom had known each other since before the groom's deployment to Manchukuo. The story of how they came back into the same room — and onto the same register — is the spine of this archive.
