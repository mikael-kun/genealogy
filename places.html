Where they lived

Places

所縁の地
paikat

Five addresses recur across the registers and the family stories. Two are in the old Yamato basin around Nara, where both Japanese families originated; one is in working-class Osaka and one in Sakai, where the families made their postwar life and ran the family shop; the last is in Bangkok, where the lives of both Keinosuke and his younger son Kazuhide ended.

Hayashikōji-chō, No. 7

奈良市 林小路町 七番地
Now: central Nara City, a short walk east of JR Nara Station.

The Kawamura family seat. The street name 林小路 means simply "the small road through the woods" — a name that hints at older topography long since paved over. In the early decades of the twentieth century the neighborhood would have consisted largely of 町家 townhouses: narrow frontages, deep lots, shared walls, built of post-and-beam 桧 hinoki and 杉 sugi framing with earthen plaster and tatami floors.

Teruhiro retains a vivid childhood memory of visiting Hayashikōji-chō: the houses standing in a row, each closely resembling the next, and the air carrying a particular smell — neither pleasant nor unpleasant, simply that smell — for which no word in any of his languages quite serves. It is, almost certainly, the layered scent of an entire vanishing way of building: hinoki resin, aging tatami rush, persimmon-tannin-treated wood, earthen walls that have absorbed and released a half-century of Nara summers. The houses themselves are likely gone; the smell still exists, in any well-preserved machiya, for anyone willing to step inside and breathe.

The neighbourhood's religious geography is unusually legible: Hayashikōji-chō held three temples of its own — 妙善寺, 霊巌院, and 円証寺 — the latter two the strongest candidates for the Kawamura's own temple, 霊巌院 (Reigan-in) being a Jōdo-shū foundation of 1591. The family would also have stood as ujiko of nearby Isagawa-jinja (率川神社), the tutelary shrine of this pocket of central Nara. Yet which temple, if any, they belonged to is unresolved: Teruhiro recalls the household as largely secular, and the one temple of his boyhood — a summer spent learning meditation and turning over questions like what is the universe — sounds more Zen than Pure Land, with chants he is sure were "definitely not nenbutsu." The street's own temples and his memory point in different directions, and the question stays open.

Ōkubo, No. 32

高市郡 畝傍町 大字大久保 三十二番地
Now: 橿原市大久保町 — Ōkubo-chō, in Kashihara City, since the merger of 1956.

The Uratani family compound, in a hamlet at the foot of Mount Unebi — one of the three sacred hills of Asuka, ringed by the imperial tumuli of the earliest legendary emperors. Toshie was born here in 1924; her son Teruhiro was born here, in the house next door at No. 31, twenty-three years later.

Sumie remained at the compound through her adult life, and it became the rural refuge to which her young nephew would escape from the density of postwar Osaka — even after her own marriage took her, in another sense, into the same kin group as the nephew himself. The address sits perhaps two kilometers north of Kashiharajingū-mae Station, an easy stop on the Kintetsu line from Namba.

The Uratani have not wholly left Ōkubochō: a house in the old family row — at the modern address 31-12 — still carries the Uratani nameplate. Around it the hamlet's old anchors survive intact: the village temple 国源寺 Kokugenji, a Pure Land (Jōdo-shū) foundation that is the modern successor to the ancient Ōkubo-dera — a Hakuhō-period temple named in the Nihon Shoki under the year 686 — and, a few steps away, the village shrine 大久保神社 Ōkubo-jinja. As danka of Kokugenji and ujiko of the shrine, the family would have marked its births, deaths, and festivals within this one small, walkable cluster across many generations. (Whether the surviving 31-12 corresponds to the old No. 32 or to the adjoining No. 31 is still to be pinned.)

Shin-Imazato

大阪市 生野区 新今里町 五丁目八十七番地 ／ 四丁目八十八番地
Now: Ikuno-ku, Osaka — south of the Tsuruhashi area, the historic heart of the Korean-Japanese community.

Where Keinosuke and Toshie made their postwar life. The address shifts in the registers between Shin-Imazato 5-chōme No. 87 (in the late 1940s and 1950s) and 4-chōme No. 88 (from the 1970s onward), the two evidently within the same small block. Both their sons established their post-marriage household registers at the latter address, which suggests it was the family home in its later, more permanent form.

It was here, too, that the family kept its trade: Keinosuke and Toshie ran a shoemaker's shop, the leather shoes ranged along the walls, and it was at that bench that the young Teruhiro learned to sew. The shop, and what it meant for the son who would later cross Siberia to paint, is told on the Kawamura page.

Ikuno-ku in the 1950s and '60s was a working-class district of small workshops, modest housing, and the long-established Zainichi Korean community whose presence still shapes the neighborhood today.

Sakai 堺

大阪府 堺市
Now: a city of its own, south of central Osaka, today merged into the Osaka metropolitan area.

The site of the family's 角打ち kaku-uchi shop — a small standing-counter sake-and-beer establishment run by Sumie's Kawamura husband. The kaku-uchi was a particularly Kansai institution, growing out of liquor wholesalers' habit of letting customers drink at the corner of the shop, glass in hand, no chairs offered. Sakai itself has its own long history — once one of Japan's great merchant cities, the Sakai of the sixteenth century rivaled Osaka, and a tradition of small artisan and merchant trades survives to the present.

In the postwar decades, the shop became a daily meeting-place where the two strands of the extended family overlapped: Sumie's household, Keinosuke and Toshie's household, the cousins of the next generation. Teruhiro retains memories of being taken on shop deliveries as a boy, and of practicing judo with one of the older cousins from this side of the family. The shop is no longer there.

Ratchathewi, Bangkok

タイ王国 バンコク都 ラチャテウィー区
Central Bangkok, just east of the Chao Phraya river.

Where Keinosuke died on 11 August 2004, at 1:20 in the afternoon. The death was registered by Kazuhide from the family's Osaka office.

For a long time the reason Bangkok appeared in the register at all was unexplained. It is now part of the family's record: Keinosuke's younger son, Kazuhide, had settled in the city with his own family, and took his father in after Toshie's death in 2003. Keinosuke died there the following year — and Kazuhide himself died in Bangkok not long after. That final chapter is told on the Kawamura page.
